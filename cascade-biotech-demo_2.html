The complement system as a therapeutic target

Cascade Biotechnology is developing therapeutics that use the body's complement system to address diseases and disorders such as Alzheimer's disease, schizophrenia, myasthenia gravis, neuromyelitis optica, cancer, age-related macular degeneration, paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobinuria, atypical hemolytic uremic syndrome, and others.

The complement system is an important part of the innate immune system, which helps protect the host against infection and connects innate and adaptive immune systems. It consists of more than 30 proteins activated through a series of proteolytic cleavages.

While complement proteins serve an important regulatory role in inflammation, over-activation of complement ultimately leads to disease states. Inhibiting overactive complement offers one strategy for managing — and potentially preventing — a range of diseases and disorders.

Complement Biology

How the complement cascade works

The following explains the full complement cascade — the biological basis for every therapeutic program at Cascade. Click any point to expand the detail.

01 What is the complement system? ▼

The complement system is an important part of the innate immune system, which helps to protect the host against infection and connects innate and adaptive immune systems. Complement consists of more than 30 proteins that are activated through a series of proteolytic cleavages, which in turn activate proteins in the next step of the complement cascade.

02 The three activation pathways ▼

There are three complement activation pathways:

Classical Pathway — activated by the presence of antibodies on the cell surface.

Lectin Pathway — activated by the recognition of certain carbohydrates on cell surfaces.

Alternative Pathway — constantly activated by spontaneous conformational changes to C3, the most common complement protein.

All three activation pathways come together through C3 activation. C3 activation allows for the activation of another complement protein, C5, that initiates the terminal complement pathway. This pathway forms the macromolecular Membrane Attack Complex (MAC), which destroys invading pathogens by punching holes in their cellular membrane.

03 How over-activation leads to disease ▼

While complement proteins serve an important regulatory role in inflammation, over-activation of complement ultimately leads to disease states. The complement system, when dysregulated, can attack the body's own tissues — driving conditions ranging from renal disease and retinal degeneration to neurodegenerative disorders and autoimmune conditions.

Inhibiting overactive complement offers one strategy for managing and potentially preventing a wide range of diseases and disorders. Cascade's platform targets multiple points in this cascade to provide precise, tissue-specific complement modulation.

04 Complement's role in the adaptive immune response ▼

Complement activation is regulated by a series of approximately 30 plasma and membrane proteins participating in classical, alternative, and lectin pathways. The classical pathway is activated by immune complexes containing antigen and IgM or complement-fixing IgG. The alternative pathway is activated by foreign pathogens and polymeric IgA. The lectin pathway is initiated by binding of mannose-binding lectin to microbial pathogens.

Although initiated differently, all three pathways converge at the cleavage of C3 by specific convertase enzymes followed by the generation of the MAC. Complement is important in both innate and adaptive immunity — it is the effector system for the primary and secondary antibody responses of B cells.

Kemper C, Atkinson JP. T-cell regulation: with complements from innate immunity. Nat Rev Immunol. 2007;7(1):9–18. | Morgan BP and Kavanaugh D. Semin Immunopathol. 2018 Jan;40(1):1-2.

Complement resolves immunological problems, yet over-activation may lead to the following disease categories:

Renal: Diabetic Nephropathy, Lupus Nephritis, Atypical Hemolytic Uremic Syndrome (aHUS)

Retinal · CB201

Age-Related Macular Degeneration

Age-related macular degeneration (AMD) is the leading cause of irreversible vision loss among the elderly, accounting for 8.7% of blindness worldwide. Globally, the total number of patients with any type of AMD is expected to increase over the next 25 years to 288 million affected individuals (Wong et al., 2014).
The disease is characterized by a gradual loss of central vision due to photoreceptor cell degeneration in the center of the retina (the macula). Photoreceptors are in close contact with the retinal pigment epithelium (RPE), which supports photoreceptor function and maintains retinal homeostasis. In AMD, this natural RPE function is disturbed, resulting in accumulation of retinal waste products called drusen underneath the RPE.
Two forms of advanced AMD are distinguished: (1) Neovascular AMD, characterized by infiltration of abnormal blood vessels into the retina — when they break, leakage leads to sudden vision loss; and (2) Geographic Atrophy, the result of gradual degeneration of the RPE and photoreceptors. No treatment is available for the majority of early, intermediate, or geographic atrophy AMD cases.
The complement system plays a central role in the etiology of AMD. Proteins involved in inflammation and immune-associated responses, including components of the complement system, were found within drusen (Hageman et al., 2001; Johnson et al., 2001; Mullins et al., 2001). Twin studies showed high concordance for AMD, with heritability estimated as high as 45–70%.
The first genome-wide association study (GWAS) for AMD in 2005 identified a highly associated genetic variant in complement factor H (CFH; Tyr402His) (Klein et al., 2005), corroborated by three additional studies. Subsequent genetic studies identified disease-associated variants in CFI, C3, C2, C9, CFB, and vitronectin (VTN). A common haplotype carrying a deletion of complement factor H related genes CFHR1 and CFHR3 was found to be protective for AMD.

Cognitive · CB801

Alzheimer's Disease

Cascade's approach to Alzheimer's disease targets complement-mediated synaptic pruning — the mechanism by which the complement system, when dysregulated, contributes to the progressive loss of synaptic connections in the AD brain.

During brain development, the complement system is needed for synaptic pruning — the elimination of excess synapses. The complement system may also facilitate synaptic pruning in neurodegenerative diseases, but in Alzheimer's disease, the amount of pruning becomes pathological.
Complement proteins C1q and C3 tag synapses for elimination. Microglia expressing complement receptors execute this elimination. In AD, this process is dysregulated and excessive — contributing to the synaptic loss that underlies cognitive decline.

Brucato FH, Benjamin DE. Synaptic Pruning in Alzheimer's Disease: Role of the Complement System. Global Journal of Medical Research F: Diseases 2020; Volume 20 Issue 6 Version 1.0. ISSN: 2249-4618.

Cognitive · CB701

Prodromal Schizophrenia

Schizophrenia is a lifelong psychiatric disorder that often strikes in the late teens or early twenties. There is a strong genetic component significantly shaped by environmental factors. The disease involves excessive loss of gray matter (Cannon TD, et al., 2002; 2015) and reduced numbers of synaptic structures on neurons (Garey LJ, et al. J Neurol Neurosurg Psychiatry. 1998; 65:446–453), and is associated with decreased dendritic spine density on prefrontal cortical pyramidal neurons (Glausier JR, Lewis DA. Neuroscience. 2013; 251:90–107).
The cause and mechanisms of schizophrenia are not well understood. More than 100 loci in the human genome contain SNP haplotypes that associate with schizophrenia risk (Schizophrenia Working Group of the Psychiatric Genomics Consortium. Nature. 2014; 511:421–427). There is an association between schizophrenia and genetic markers across the Major Histocompatibility Complex (MHC) locus — known for its role in immunity.
Studies have shown that strongly associated markers are near a complex, multi-allelic form of genome variation that affects the C4 gene encoding complement component 4. Schizophrenia is also associated with CSMD1, which encodes a regulator of C4 (Sekar et al., Nature 2016, Feb 11; 530(7589):177–183).
Very recently, C5 levels in CSF were found to be abnormally high in several psychiatric disorders, including bipolar disorder and schizophrenia (Ishii T et al. Biochem Biophys Res Commun. 2018 Mar 4;497(2):683–688). Increased synapse elimination by microglia has been observed in schizophrenia patient-derived models of synaptic pruning (Sellgren CM, et al. Nature Neuroscience 2019, 22(3):374–385).

Sekar et al. (2016) Schizophrenia risk from complex variation of complement component 4. Nature. 530(7589): 177–183.

Kamitaki N, et al. (2020) Complement genes contribute sex-biased vulnerability in diverse disorders. Nature.

Woo JJ, et al. (2020) The complement system in schizophrenia: where are we now and what's next? Molecular Psychiatry 25(1):114–130.

Autoimmune · CNS

Multiple Sclerosis

High levels of complement system factors are found in blood and cerebrospinal fluid of MS patients, suggesting that the system is actively contributing to disease progression.
Studies have shown that increased gray matter lesions predict disease course. During brain development, the complement system is needed for synaptic pruning. The complement system may also facilitate synaptic pruning in neurodegenerative diseases, but in MS, the amount of synaptic pruning is incorrect — driven by unregulated complement activation.
Studies have confirmed this unregulated pruning by showing the presence of certain activated complement factors along with a lack of molecules that normally control them. A large set of complement factors, receptors, and regulatory molecules were present in the brains of deceased MS patients. High levels of activated complement can be found in gray matter lesions in both the surface and deeper brain regions.
The extent of nerve cell loss in a brain area was linked to the numbers of cells with complement factors in that area. Complement over-activation may contribute to the worsening pathology that underlies the irreversible progression of MS.

Watkins et al. (2016) Complement is activated in progressive multiple sclerosis cortical grey matter lesions. Journal of Neuroinflammation 13:161.

Autoimmune · CB301

Neuromyelitis Optica

Neuromyelitis optica (NMO) is an autoimmune inflammatory disease that selectively targets the optic nerves and spinal cord, leading to blindness and paralysis (Ratelade J, et al., Acta Neuropathologica 2013). Since NMO patients often present with demyelinating CNS lesions, it was long considered a variant of MS; however, recent data suggest its pathogenesis may be different (Jurynczyk et al., J Neurology, Neurosurgery, and Psychiatry 2015).
The immunopathology of NMO includes restricted demyelination and inflammation of the optic nerves and several spinal segments. In contrast to multiple sclerosis, autoantibodies against aquaporin-4 (AQP4) expressed on astrocytic end-feet have been exclusively detected in sera of NMOSD patients.
Several lines of evidence suggest that anti-AQP4 autoantibodies are pathogenic. Anti-AQP4 Ab binding to astrocytes initiates two parallel complementary reactions: (1) complement cytotoxicity via C5b-9 complex formation, and (2) reversal of the astrocyte glutamate pump by C5a pre-activated neutrophils, resulting in extracellular glutamate accumulation.

Piatek P, et al. (2018) C5a Pre-activated Neutrophils Are Critical for Autoimmune-Induced Astrocyte Dysregulation In Neuromyelitis Optica Spectrum Disorder. Front Immunol. doi:10.3389/fimmu.2018.01694.

Mediators of Inflammation Volume 2018, Article ID 4187347 — Combination Treatment of C16 Peptide and Angiopoietin-1 Alleviates Neuromyelitis Optica in an Experimental Model.

Myasthenia Gravis

Myasthenia gravis (MG) is primarily caused by antibodies directed towards the skeletal muscle acetylcholine receptor, leading to muscle weakness. Although these antibodies may induce compromise of neuromuscular transmission by blocking acetylcholine receptor function, the predominant mechanism of injury is complement-mediated lysis of the postsynaptic membrane.
Complement plays a central role in EAMG and MG pathogenesis, as well as an important role in T- and B-cell function. Complement inhibitor-based therapy has great potential to treat human MG. Activation of the complement system protects the host against invading pathogens through cell lysis, opsonization with complement fragments, chemotaxis of inflammatory cells, and formation of the MAC.
In the adaptive immune response, complement is the effector system for the primary and secondary antibody responses of B cells. All three complement pathways converge at the cleavage of C3 by specific convertase enzymes followed by generation of the MAC. The existence of a new activation pathway — generation of C5a through a coagulation pathway involving thrombin in the absence of C3 — has also been proposed (Huber-Lang M et al. Nat Med. 2006;12(6):682–687).

Huda R, Fritzinger DC, Finnegan PF, et al. (2011) Complement depletion with humanized Cobra Venom Factor (CVF) improves the severity of Experimental Autoimmune Myasthenia Gravis (EAMG). Mol Immunol 48:1712.

Kusner et al. (2008) Effect of complement and its regulation on myasthenia gravis pathogenesis. Expert Rev Clin Immunol 4(1):43–52.

Autoimmune · CB901

Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis (ALS)

Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS) is a fatal progressive neurodegenerative disease with no available therapy. Components of the innate immune system are activated in the spinal cord and central nervous system of ALS patients.
Studies in the SOD1G93A mouse show deposition of C1q and C3/C3b at the motor end-plate before neurological symptoms are apparent, suggesting that complement activation precedes neurodegeneration in this model. This makes complement activation an early and actionable target — potentially enabling intervention before irreversible motor neuron loss occurs.
Analyzing post-mortem tissue of ALS donors may lead to a better understanding of the complement system's role and its activation at the motor end-plate in human ALS.

Bahia, Idrissi, Bosch, Ramaglia, Aronica, Baas, Troost D (2016) Complement activation at the motor end-plates in amyotrophic lateral sclerosis. J Neuroinflammation. Apr 7;13(1):72. doi: 10.1186/s12974-016-0538-2.

Blood Disorder

Paroxysmal Nocturnal Hemoglobinuria (PNH)

When the complement system is activated, it triggers events leading to cleavage of protein C5. Once C5 is cleaved, events occur that propagate the formation of the membrane attack complex (MAC). This MAC generates pores in cells, ultimately leading to the cell's demise.
In PNH, the complement regulator proteins CD55 and CD59 — which normally sit on the outer membrane of cells so the complement system recognizes them as "self" — are missing. This is because of a mutation in a gene called PIG-A, which causes the cell's inability to form the GPI anchor that attaches these regulatory proteins to the cell surface.
When the complement system becomes highly active from infections, surgery, or similar events, it creates increased cell death in those cells missing this protein shield. The MAC makes holes and pores on the red blood cell surface, releasing hemoglobin intravascularly — this is hemolysis. Eventually the cell completely ruptures, releasing all free hemoglobin.
Consequences include hemolytic anemia, thrombosis due to inflammation, and kidney problems because free hemoglobin filters through the kidney tubules — leading to hemoglobinuria (red urine). Continued hemoglobinuria can lead to kidney damage. High-sensitivity flow cytometry can now identify small PNH clones — enabling classification of "subclinical PNH" where patients may not yet present with hemolysis but warrant monitoring.

Expert Rev Hematol. 2014 October; 7(5):583–598.

Jamile Shammo, MD. Interviews with the Experts: The Complement System in PNH. Aplastic Anemia and MDS International Foundation (AA MDS). 2013/2016.

Fritzinger DC, et al. (2008) A novel concept for the treatment of PNH: complement depletion with a human C3 derivative prevents lysis of PNH erythrocytes. Mol Immunol 45:4177.

Renal Disease

Atypical Hemolytic Uremic Syndrome (aHUS)

The complement cascade provides an important line of defense against invasive pathogens. However, the complement system also causes kidney injury in a variety of different diseases. Complement activation is particularly important in the development of aHUS and C3 glomerulopathy. Complement inhibition is effective for treatment of aHUS, and complement inhibitors will likely be tested in other kidney diseases in the future.
While the role of the complement system in the pathogenesis of many kidney diseases is well established, there is not a simple algorithm for identifying which patients should be treated with complement inhibitors or for how long complement inhibition should be continued.
Complement cascade is involved in several renal diseases and in renal transplantation. Study of complement involvement has led to a reclassification of some renal diseases — moving from a histopathological to a physiopathological classification. Many targets such as C1, C3, C5a and C5aR are currently the subject of national or international clinical trials.

Joshua M. Thurman (2015) Complement in Kidney Disease: Core Curriculum 2015. Am J Kidney Dis. 65(1):156–168.

Salvadori M, Rosso G, Bertoni E (2015) Complement involvement in kidney diseases: From physiopathology to therapeutical targeting. World J Nephrol. 4(2):169–184.

Cardiovascular

Cardiac & Myocardial Ischemia

Regarding inflammation as one mediator of ischemia-reperfusion (I/R) injury, experimental and clinical studies have shown that reperfusion after transient ischemia results in activation of endothelial cells, the contact system, and the complement system — with attraction of neutrophils to the site of infarction.
The complement system is a major component of innate immunity, involved in both recognition and response to pathogens. It is further implicated in an increasing number of homeostatic and disease processes such as immune complex catabolism, clearance of dead and dying cells, and the modulation of adaptive immune responses.
Three pathways activate the complement cascade: classical, alternative, and lectin. After initiation, these pathways converge at the level of cleavage and activation of complement component C3, which leads to generation of the anaphylatoxins (C3a, C5a) and the membrane attack complex (MAC; C5b-9).

sC3 Platform Science

Stabilized C3 — our core molecule

The sC3 platform represents over three decades of protein engineering. The following details the key data sets and design decisions underlying our lead therapeutic molecule.

Complement Depletion & Disease Models

Cascade has established model systems demonstrating that complement depletion with sC3 protects against disease in preclinical models of AMD, myasthenia gravis, and myocardial ischemia-reperfusion injury. These models validated the therapeutic concept across multiple disease indications before advancing to in vivo studies.

Human C3 Immunogenicity Design

Early humanization of cobra venom factor (CVF) created a molecule with potential immunogenicity concerns at position C3-1496. Cascade's protein engineering team used advanced immunogenicity prediction tools to redesign this region, creating a molecule with substantially reduced immunogenic potential while retaining full complement-depleting activity.

C3b Crystal Structure — Stabilizing Amino Acid Changes

Using the C3b crystal structure, Cascade identified precise amino acid positions that, when changed, stabilize the C3 molecule in its active configuration (sC3). These structural insights enabled rational design of a molecule with superior pharmacokinetic properties compared to prior complement-depleting proteins.

Increased Catalytic Efficiency & Potency

sC3 was designed for increased catalytic efficiency, potency, and better pharmacokinetic (pK) properties. The molecule demonstrates superior complement depletion compared to humanized cobra venom factor (CVF) analogs, with a more favorable PK profile enabling less frequent dosing and potentially lower therapeutic doses.

sC3 Depletes Complement More Potently than CVF

Head-to-head studies demonstrate that sC3 depletes complement more potently than cobra venom factor. This superior potency, combined with the improved serum stability and reduced immunogenicity profile, makes sC3 a superior therapeutic candidate to prior complement-depleting biologics.

Serum Stability & Degradation Resistance

A key engineering achievement is that sC3 is significantly more stable in serum than prior complement-depleting proteins, and resists degradation by serum proteases. This stability profile supports a longer duration of action and enables more practical dosing regimens for chronic disease indications.

Gene Therapy Strategy

Rather than requiring repeated protein injections, Cascade's gene therapy strategy uses viral delivery to have cells continuously produce sC3 locally at the site of disease. This approach is particularly suited to diseases like AMD (retinal) and diabetic nephropathy (renal), where local tissue-specific delivery can maximize efficacy and minimize systemic off-target effects.

Sub-Retinal Injections & RPE Cell Targeting

For retinal gene therapy, Cascade has demonstrated that sub-retinal injections of lentiviral particles can deliver the sC3 gene to retinal pigment epithelium (RPE) cells. These cells, central to AMD pathology, can then continuously produce sC3 locally — providing sustained complement depletion exactly where it is needed.

Vector Design & Lentivirus Synthesis

Cascade has completed gene therapy vector design for sC3, synthesized the lentivirus, and successfully infected RPE cells. HEK293 co-transfection studies confirmed that the sC3 coding sequence results in functional protein secretion. These milestones confirm the feasibility of the gene therapy approach.

Intellectual Property

Cascade has developed a portfolio of intellectual property surrounding the sC3 platform, covering the stabilized C3 molecule itself, its gene sequences, the gene therapy delivery vectors, and methods of treatment across multiple disease indications. This IP portfolio provides a strong foundation for partnership and licensing discussions.
